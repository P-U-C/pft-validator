## Thesis: The Search-to-LLM Transition in Investment Decision-Making

### The Shift

For two decades, the investment research funnel looked the same: Google → analyst reports → Bloomberg terminal → thesis → trade. Information asymmetry was geographic and institutional — if you had access to better data sources, you had edge.

That funnel is collapsing. Increasingly, the first step is not a search query but an LLM prompt: "What are the strongest arguments for X sector?" or "Analyze this earnings report against consensus." The implications are structural, not incremental.

### Why LLMs Change the Funnel Shape

**Traditional search returns links.** You get ten blue results, each requiring you to read, evaluate credibility, extract signal, and synthesize across sources. The bottleneck is human bandwidth — a competent analyst can process maybe 20-30 sources per day deeply.

**LLM outputs return synthesis.** You get a pre-digested argument with evidence marshaled and counterpoints raised. The bottleneck shifts from *finding information* to *evaluating the quality of synthesis*. This is a fundamentally different skill.

The consequences:

1. **Information asymmetry flattens horizontally but deepens vertically.** Everyone gets the same quality of surface-level synthesis. Edge now comes from knowing what *questions* to ask, having proprietary data to feed in, or having the domain expertise to evaluate when the LLM's synthesis is wrong. The value of the prompt > the value of the answer.

2. **Speed of conviction formation accelerates.** What took an analyst a week of research now takes minutes. This compresses the time between "interesting signal" and "position entry." Markets move faster. First-mover advantage shifts from "who can read fastest" to "who can formulate the right question first."

3. **The credibility crisis inverts.** Google search suffered from SEO gaming — bad content ranked because it was optimized for algorithms, not truth. LLM outputs suffer from training data bias — the synthesis reflects whatever the training corpus weighted. But crucially, the user *can't see the sources* by default. In search, you could at least evaluate the domain. In LLM output, the synthesis looks authoritative regardless of provenance.

### Two Sources, One Signal

Two independent analyses confirm this transition is underway:

**Alex Imas** (["What Will Be Scarce?"](https://aleximas.substack.com/p/what-will-be-scarce)) argues that AI commoditizes cognitive labor. Applied to investment: the "research analyst" function — reading reports, building models, writing memos — is exactly the cognitive labor being commoditized. What remains scarce is *judgment*: knowing when the model is wrong, having conviction to act against the synthesis, understanding context the LLM can't access.

His experimental data is directly relevant: willingness-to-pay doubled for exclusive products, and human-made art carried a 44% exclusivity premium vs. 21% for AI-generated. In investment terms: **human-originated research with transparent provenance carries a premium over LLM-generated analysis**, even when the LLM analysis is objectively better. People pay for the signal that a human with skin in the game did the work.

**@moneyfetishist** (["The Leisure Infrastructure Playbook"](https://x.com/moneyfetishist/status/2045973383280787940)) demonstrates this in practice: AI capex running at ~$650B/yr, yet Bain 2025 shows all net luxury growth came from physical experiences, not digital goods. Investment capital is already flowing away from the AI-commoditized (SaaS, content, routine analysis) and toward the AI-resistant (supply-constrained physical assets, relational services). The smart money is acting on the thesis before the thesis is articulated.

### What This Means for On-Chain Intelligence

Post Fiat sits at the intersection of this transition:

**1. The Herald as LLM-native research source.**

The Hive Herald isn't competing with Bloomberg or analyst reports. It's competing for inclusion in the context window. When an AI agent is asked "What's happening on PFT?", the Herald's structured daily output (top earners, airdrop data, velocity metrics, sybil updates) is exactly the format LLMs can ingest and reason about. The Herald doesn't need human readers — it needs to be the canonical machine-readable source for PFT network state.

This reframes the subscription value. A human subscriber pays 1,000 PFT to read the Herald. An AI agent's operator pays to ensure their model has accurate, current PFT data. The second market is larger.

**2. On-chain provenance as credibility layer.**

The LLM credibility crisis — synthesis without visible sources — creates demand for verifiable provenance. Post Fiat's on-chain task verification creates an auditable chain: human request → human work → on-chain proof → reviewable artifact. When an LLM cites a Post Fiat analysis, the provenance is checkable on-chain. This is the answer to "how do I know this synthesis isn't hallucinated?"

The 733-bot network discovery illustrates this perfectly. An LLM trained on raw PFT transaction data would see 871 "active contributors." The sybil detection work reduces that to 130 real humans. The on-chain provenance of *who is real* is the difference between actionable intelligence and noise.

**3. Task node as the anti-commodity research layer.**

If commodity research is what LLMs produce for free, premium research is what they *can't* produce: novel thesis generation grounded in proprietary observation, human judgment about what matters, and conviction expressed through capital deployment (earning PFT by doing the work, not just writing about it). The task node's structure — propose, execute, verify on-chain, get rewarded — creates exactly the skin-in-the-game signal that distinguishes human-originated research from LLM commodity output.

## Dislikes

1. **No visibility into task generation logic.** The system converted "I want a collaborative task" into a specific proposal with 3,000 PFT reward, but there's no reasoning trace. Was the reward calibrated to prior similar tasks? Was the feedback-collection shape chosen because alpha coverage density was low? Exposing this reasoning would help producers write better requests and would itself be valuable training data for AI agents evaluating task quality.

2. **No cross-pollination between producers.** With 5+ producers already rewarded, their submissions should inform remaining contributors. Instead each producer works blind. The task is called "collaborative" but the execution is parallel-solo. Showing anonymized scenario labels or thesis summaries from completed submissions would create genuine collaboration.

3. **Deadline asymmetry is confusing.** Task generated April 20, deadline April 24. But some producers joined April 16-17. The timeline metadata doesn't clearly distinguish task versions, making it hard for new contributors to gauge the actual work window or understand the task's evolution.

4. **Uniform reward doesn't signal quality tiers.** 3,000 PFT regardless of whether the submission is a surface pass or deep original research. Earlier tasks had different reward calibrations (UX stress test: 1,500 PFT). There's no mechanism to signal that deeper work earns proportionally more, which biases toward minimum-viable submissions — the opposite of what alpha feedback collection needs.

## Improvements

1. **Expose task generation reasoning as structured metadata.** When the system generates a task proposal, emit a JSON reasoning trace: `{"trigger": "collaborative_request", "shape_selected": "feedback_collection", "reward_basis": "median_collaborative_3000", "rationale": "alpha_feedback_density_below_threshold"}`. This is training data for AI agents AND transparency for producers.

2. **Add a "prior submissions" panel for collaborative tasks.** After N producers complete, show scenario labels and one-line summaries. This enables genuine collaboration: later producers can fill coverage gaps instead of duplicating angles. The task becomes more valuable as participation increases.

3. **Implement structured reward tiers.** Define in the verification criteria: "Base (meets four headings + URL): 3,000 PFT. Extended (includes original research/data + cross-references): 1.5x multiplier." Structural, not subjective — the tier criteria are checkable by the same verification logic.

4. **Publish Herald data in a machine-readable API endpoint.** The thesis above argues the Herald's biggest market is AI agents, not human readers. A `/api/herald/latest.json` endpoint with structured fields (top_earners, airdrops, velocity, sybil_count) would make PFT intelligence directly consumable by AI agents without scraping HTML. This is the product implication of the LLM-as-investment-funnel shift.

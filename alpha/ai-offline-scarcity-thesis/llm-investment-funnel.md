# LLM Outputs as the New Investment Funnel

*How AI agents are replacing search as the primary channel for investment decisions — and why this restructures information asymmetry, not just speed.*

---

## The Shift

For two decades, the investment research funnel looked the same: Google → analyst reports → Bloomberg terminal → thesis → trade. Information asymmetry was geographic and institutional — if you had access to better data sources, you had edge. A fund in New York with a Bloomberg subscription and three sector analysts had a structural advantage over a retail investor with a browser. The game was access.

That funnel is collapsing. Increasingly, the first step is not a search query but an LLM prompt: *"What are the strongest arguments for investing in X sector?"* or *"Analyze this earnings report against consensus and flag surprises."* This isn't a faster version of the old funnel. It's a different funnel entirely, and the implications are structural.

## Why LLMs Change the Funnel Shape

**Traditional search returns links.** You get ten blue results, each requiring you to click through, read, evaluate the source's credibility, extract the relevant signal, and mentally synthesize across 20-30 tabs. The bottleneck is human bandwidth. A competent analyst can deeply process maybe 20-30 sources per day. A great analyst processes the same 30 but notices the one contradiction that everyone else missed.

**LLM outputs return synthesis.** You get a pre-digested argument with evidence marshaled, counterpoints raised, and a conclusion suggested — all in seconds. The bottleneck shifts from *finding information* to *evaluating the quality of synthesis*. This is a fundamentally different cognitive skill. The analyst who was great at reading 30 reports isn't necessarily great at detecting when a plausible-sounding synthesis is subtly wrong.

Three consequences follow:

### 1. Information Asymmetry Flattens Horizontally, Deepens Vertically

Everyone — the New York fund, the retail investor in Manila, the solo GP running a $5M vehicle — now gets the same quality of surface-level synthesis. The Bloomberg terminal's edge erodes because the LLM has already read every 10-K, every earnings transcript, every sell-side note.

But a new asymmetry opens *vertically*. Edge now comes from:

- **Knowing what questions to ask.** The LLM gives excellent answers to mediocre questions. The alpha is in the question, not the answer. A prompt like "analyze NVIDIA earnings" produces commodity output. A prompt like "what would NVIDIA's gross margin look like if their top 3 hyperscaler customers built competing silicon within 18 months, and what historical precedent exists for vertical integration timelines in semiconductor supply chains?" produces differentiated output. **The value of the prompt exceeds the value of the answer.**

- **Having proprietary data to feed in.** The LLM's training data is public. Anyone can query it. But if you feed in your firm's proprietary channel checks, your network's real-time observations, or sensor data that isn't in the training corpus, the synthesis becomes unique. The LLM becomes a reasoning engine over your private information advantage, not a substitute for one.

- **Having the domain depth to evaluate when the synthesis is wrong.** LLMs are confidently wrong at a rate that's extremely difficult to detect without expertise. They present the consensus view fluently. But consensus is where returns go to die. The investor who can read an LLM's synthesis and say "this is wrong because it's weighting a lagging indicator" has an edge that scales with the adoption of LLMs by less sophisticated participants.

### 2. Speed of Conviction Formation Accelerates

What took an analyst a week of research — reading filings, building a model, talking to industry contacts, writing a memo — now takes minutes for the synthesis step. This compresses the time between "interesting signal" and "position entry."

The first-order effect is obvious: markets move faster. The second-order effect is subtler and more important: **the premium on pre-existing mental models increases.** When you could spend a week on research, you could afford to build your thesis from scratch. When the synthesis arrives in 30 seconds, the investor who already has a framework — who has been thinking about a sector for years — can evaluate and act faster than someone encountering the space for the first time.

This inverts the common assumption that AI levels the playing field. It *does* level the information field. But it *steepens* the interpretation field. Experience and domain conviction become more valuable, not less, because the time window for acting on new information compresses.

### 3. The Credibility Crisis Inverts

Google search suffered from SEO gaming. Bad content ranked because it was optimized for algorithms, not truth. But at least you could see the source domain and make a judgment: *Wall Street Journal, probably reliable. Random blogspot, probably not.*

LLM outputs suffer from a different credibility problem. The synthesis reflects whatever the training corpus weighted — which skews toward consensus, recency, and volume of discussion rather than accuracy. Crucially, **the user can't see the sources by default.** The synthesis looks authoritative regardless of provenance. A confidently wrong answer looks identical to a confidently right one.

This creates a paradox: the tool that's supposed to improve research quality actually degrades the user's ability to evaluate what they're reading. In the search era, source evaluation was tedious but possible. In the LLM era, source evaluation requires actively demanding citations and then checking them — a step most users skip because the synthesis *feels* complete.

For investment decisions, this is dangerous. Markets are adversarial environments. When most participants are using the same LLMs with the same training data, the consensus synthesis becomes the consensus position. And the consensus position is, by definition, already priced. **The LLM's greatest strength — synthesizing the consensus view — is precisely what makes it a poor source of alpha.**

## Who Wins, Who Loses

The transition creates clear winners and losers:

**Winners:**

- **Operators with proprietary data pipelines.** If your edge is information the LLM hasn't seen — real-time on-chain data, sensor networks, channel checks, private market deal flow — the LLM amplifies your advantage by letting you reason about your data faster.

- **Deep domain specialists.** The generalist analyst who could "cover" 40 stocks by reading reports is being replaced. The specialist who has spent 15 years in semiconductor supply chains and can smell when a synthesis is wrong has never been more valuable.

- **Question architects.** People who are excellent at decomposing complex questions into precise sub-questions that extract non-obvious signal from LLMs. This is a skill that barely existed two years ago. It's now a core competency.

- **Provenance infrastructure.** Tools and platforms that can verify *where* a piece of analysis came from, *who* did the underlying work, and *whether* it's been validated. In a world of confident synthesis without visible sources, verifiable provenance becomes a moat.

**Losers:**

- **Generalist research coverage.** The sell-side analyst publishing "company X beat on revenue, missed on EPS, maintain overweight" is producing exactly what LLMs produce for free. This function is commoditized.

- **Access-based edge.** If your advantage was having a Bloomberg terminal and the other guy didn't, that advantage is gone. The terminal's data is in the training set.

- **Volume-based content producers.** SEO-optimized financial content farms, newsletter mills churning out "top 10 stocks to buy" — all of this is commodity LLM output now. The market for it collapses.

- **Slow conviction.** If your process requires a week of research before forming a view, you're systematically late in a world where conviction forms in minutes. The process itself needs to change.

## The Scarce Complements

Two recent analyses from different angles confirm what becomes scarce — and therefore valuable — when cognitive output is commoditized:

Alex Imas ([*"What Will Be Scarce?"*](https://aleximas.substack.com/p/what-will-be-scarce)) shows that when AI commoditizes production, economic value migrates toward *relational* goods — work and experiences where human presence is the product. His experimental data is striking: willingness to pay doubled for exclusive products, and human-made art commanded a 44% exclusivity premium versus only 21% for AI-generated art. Applied to investment: **human-originated research with skin in the game carries a premium over LLM-generated analysis, even when the LLM output is objectively more comprehensive.** The provenance signal — a human with capital at risk did this work — is itself valuable.

@moneyfetishist ([*"The Leisure Infrastructure Playbook"*](https://x.com/moneyfetishist/status/2045973383280787940)) demonstrates this in capital flows: AI capex is running at ~$650B/year (~2% of US GDP), yet Bain's 2025 luxury report shows that *all* net luxury market growth since 2023 came from experiences, not goods. The luxury consumer base shrank from 400M to 340M but spend-per-person rose. Capital is already flowing from the AI-commoditized (SaaS, content, routine analysis) toward the AI-resistant (supply-constrained physical assets, exclusive access, relational services). The smart money is acting on this thesis before most people have articulated it.

The connection to the LLM investment funnel: **if LLM-generated research is commodity, then human-generated research with verifiable provenance is luxury.** And the data shows people pay more — significantly more — for the luxury version, even when the commodity version is adequate.

## Actionable Implications

For anyone building in this environment — or allocating capital within it:

**The question is the product, not the answer.** Build systems that help users ask better questions, not systems that give better answers to bad questions. The LLM answer to "should I buy NVIDIA?" is worthless. The LLM answer to a carefully constructed multi-part query with proprietary context is genuinely useful. The margin is in the prompt engineering layer.

**Proprietary data becomes the only durable moat.** If your investment process can be replicated by feeding the same public data into an LLM, you don't have a process — you have an illusion. The firms that win are those combining LLM reasoning with data the LLM hasn't seen.

**Provenance is the new credibility.** In a world flooded with confident synthesis, the ability to prove "a human with domain expertise and capital at risk produced this analysis, and here's the on-chain record" is a differentiated signal. It's not enough to be right — you need to prove you were right for the right reasons, and that a human was in the loop.

**Build for the AI agent, not the human reader.** The next generation of investment research consumers aren't humans scrolling through PDFs. They're AI agents ingesting structured data on behalf of human decision-makers. Research products that output machine-readable structured data (JSON, typed APIs) will capture more distribution than those publishing prose for human eyes. The human reads the AI's summary of your research — not your research directly.

**Short consensus, long conviction.** When everyone has the same LLM producing the same synthesis, consensus positions are more crowded than ever. The returns accrue to those who have conviction that deviates from what the LLM says — and the domain expertise to know when that deviation is signal, not noise.
